Capturing the ideas, then picking the one for Naftali

Run this as the intake process itself: let each idea get told, capture it against the same fields, then score to choose. The act of doing this in the room is the demonstration of how intake would work at SentiLink.

The qualifying inputs (what to capture per idea)

1. Candidate nameShort handle for the idea.
2. Problem / pain pointWhat is broken or slow today, and who feels it?
3. BaselineCurrent-state number: time, frequency, cost, rework. If unmeasurable, note that.
4. Desired outcomeWhat good looks like, as a measurable before and after.
5. Scope and reusabilityHow big, and does it generalize? One-off, or a primitive other teams reuse?
6. Confidence we can solve itHas a version worked before? Riskiest unknown?
7. Cost and effortEngineering-free? Time to MVP vs end-to-end? Discovery, alignment, tokens, infra, budget?
8. Data sensitivity / constraintsTouches sensitive identity or customer data? Privacy, security, regulatory gates?
9. Is AI even the right tool?Could a process change, policy fix, or basic automation solve it first?
10. Naftali fitWould this resonate as a demo? Does it show something he cares about?

Scoring the 1–10 columns: higher is always better for the pick. Note the framing on three columns: column 7 is low cost/effort (10 = cheap and fast), column 8 is low data risk (10 = no sensitive data), and column 9 is needs AI (10 = genuinely needs AI; a low score means a process or policy fix would do, which is a real outcome, just not a build-session pick).

Aligned to the process guide. Each idea enters as a Candidate and the one you choose advances to Scored, the same status taxonomy in the guide. Column A (reusable primitive) and column C (enables others) tie directly to the guide’s two mandates: a pick that is a reusable building block and teaches others as it is built serves both the build and the transform mandate at once, which is the strongest kind of Naftali demo. Field B (who adopts it, and will they) is the guide’s adoption gate, asked early.

RICE, for the final cut

ReachHow many people or how much volume it touches over a set period.
ImpactHow much it moves the needle per person or instance (massive = 3, high = 2, medium = 1, low = 0.5).
ConfidenceHow sure we are, as a percentage (100% / 80% / 50%).
EffortPerson-time to build, in person-weeks. Lower is better.

RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort. Use it as the tiebreaker once the 1–10 columns have narrowed the field. RICE rewards big, high-confidence, low-effort ideas, which is exactly what makes a strong Naftali demo.

The pick for Naftali should win on four things at once: visible value (he will care), reusability (proves the primitive-that-generalizes idea), buildability (you can actually prototype it before the meeting, ideally without engineering), and low data risk (won’t stall on security review first). A smaller idea you can fully build and show working beats a bigger one you can only describe.
